On god means I swear. It's the verbal pinky promise: whatever I just said, I'm staking something sacred on it being true. "That test was impossible, on god." "Best tacos in the city, on god." The statement comes first, the oath seals it.

## From the block to the For You Page

The phrase comes from African American Vernacular English, where swearing on God has always been the highest-truth setting available, and hip-hop carried it into the mainstream long before TikTok existed. In texts it often shrinks to ONG, same oath, fewer letters.

Its closest cousin is "no cap," and the two do the same job from different angles: no cap denies the lie, on god swears the truth. Gen Z keeps both in rotation because sincerity, ironically, needs constant re-certification. Everyone exaggerates everything, so a statement without an oath attached barely counts as a claim anymore.

## How to use it

Placement is almost always the end of the sentence, like a stamp. It works on opinions, stories, and promises, and it stacks for escalation: "on my mama" is the level above, reserved for when on god alone might not close the deal.

One warning: the oath loses value with every exaggeration you attach it to. Swear on the tacos all you want, but if you said "on god" about the last four taco places too, people start pricing in your inflation. I learned this the hard way after vouching for a movie that turned out unwatchable; the group's trust in my oaths took a full month to recover.

## on god in the wild

> "this is the best show I've seen all year, on god. episode 4 had me standing up."

> @groupchatgabe: i will actually start going to the gym this time. on god. (he will not)

> "she said she was 10 minutes away and pulled up in 9. on god she's the only reliable one."

Short oath. Big promise.
