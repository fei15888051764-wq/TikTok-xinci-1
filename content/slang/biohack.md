## What Does 'Biohack' Mean? The TikTok Wellness Trend Explained (2026)

By **GEBILAOWANG** | Published: July 7, 2026

"Biohack" is a 2025-2026 term for using lifestyle modifications, technology, supplements, or experimental methods to optimize one's physical and mental performance, health, and longevity. Shortlisted for Oxford Word of the Year 2025, it reflects the growing cultural obsession with self-optimization and the quantification of personal wellbeing.

## Why "Biohack" Captures the Self-Optimization Obsession of 2026

The term works because it frames health and wellness as a solvable engineering problem — an appealing proposition for a generation that grew up with smartphones and apps that promise to optimize everything from sleep to productivity. Unlike traditional "healthy living," which feels passive and slow, "biohacking" sounds active, experimental, and tech-forward. GEBILAOWANG's take: what's fascinating about "biohack" is how it commercializes the human body. What started as a niche subculture of tech enthusiasts measuring their own biomarkers has become a massive industry of supplements, wearables, and lifestyle programs. The language itself reveals the mindset: your body isn't an organism to be cared for — it's a system to be optimized. This framing is both empowering and potentially dangerous, as it can lead people to pursue unproven or extreme interventions in the name of "optimization."

## How the Pursuit of Peak Performance Became a Lifestyle Movement

The term "biohack" blends "biology" with "hack" — the tech-inspired idea of finding shortcuts or workarounds to optimize systems. As Oxford University Press documented when shortlisting it for Word of the Year 2025, the verb means "to attempt to improve or optimize one's physical or mental performance, health, longevity, or wellbeing by altering one's diet, exercise routine, or lifestyle, or by using other means such as drugs, supplements, or technological devices." The concept has roots in the quantified self-movement of the early 2010s, but it exploded into mainstream consciousness in 2025 when tech billionaires and Silicon Valley executives began publicly experimenting with extreme longevity protocols. PopSugar's April 2024 coverage noted that #biohack regularly trends on TikTok, where influencers share everything from cold plunge routines to intermittent fasting schedules to nootropic supplement stacks. The trend has a clear demographic skew — hashtag data shows 48% of #biohack users are aged 25-34, with men slightly outnumbering women. But experts urge caution. Dr. Michelle Loy, an integrative medicine specialist at New York-Presbyterian, told PopSugar that while some techniques are harmless, others "lack scientific evidence regarding effectiveness, and potentially carry unknown, long-term consequences."

#### FAQ

- **Q:** What older expression is this most similar to? How is it different?
  **A:** "Biohack" is closest to "self-improvement" or "health optimization," but it's much more technology-focused and data-driven. Traditional wellness emphasizes balance and intuition; biohacking emphasizes measurement, experimentation, and quantifiable results. It's also more individualistic — biohackers tend to see their bodies as unique systems requiring personalized protocols rather than following general health advice.

- **Q:** Where should I avoid using this word?
  **A:** Don't use "biohack" in medical or clinical contexts unless you're speaking casually — it's not a medical term, and healthcare professionals may find it reductive. Also avoid using it to pressure others into trying unproven supplements or extreme protocols. What works as a "biohack" for one person could be dangerous for another. The term works best for describing lifestyle experimentation, not medical interventions.

- **Q:** Is this word still fresh or already fading?
  **A:** With Oxford WOTY 2025 shortlist status, "biohack" has strong institutional recognition. GEBILAOWANG predicts it will remain in active use through 2027, though it may evolve from a trendy buzzword into a more specific technical term. The underlying behavior — using technology to optimize health — is likely to persist and grow, even if the specific word gets replaced by more precise terminology.

- **Q:** How do I explain this quickly to someone who's out of the loop?
  **A:** "It's using technology, supplements, or lifestyle changes to optimize your body and mind — like treating yourself as a computer system that can be upgraded and fine-tuned."
