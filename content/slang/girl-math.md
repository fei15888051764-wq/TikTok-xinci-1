Girl math is the art of making any purchase financially responsible through pure confidence. Pay in cash? That's free, the money already left your account. Buy something on sale? You didn't spend $40, you saved $60. Return something? Congratulations, that's income now. Cost-per-wear is the crown jewel of the system: divide the price by imagined uses and a $300 bag suddenly costs 40 cents a day.

It's budgeting as improv comedy, and everyone who does it knows exactly what they're doing. That's the joke.

## How three Kiwi radio hosts started it

The term didn't actually start on TikTok. It started on New Zealand radio. The breakfast show Fletch, Vaughan and Hayley on ZM had a running bit where they'd help callers justify purchases with creative accounting. In mid-2023, a clip about rationalizing a Dyson Airwrap went properly nuclear, pulling around 38 million views. Their TikTok exploded too. The show went from roughly 40,000 followers to 240,000. CBS News was covering the trend by September 2023.

Then came the franchise: boy math, mom math, corporate math. None of them hit like the original.

## Not everyone's a fan

The backlash showed up fast. Critics argued the joke leans on the "women are bad with money" stereotype, and a finance professor warned it could do real harm if people took the logic even half seriously. My take: that's overthinking a joke women mostly tell about themselves. The whole bit only works because the person doing the math knows it's nonsense. Nobody is out here actually believing a returned sweater is a paycheck.

> "this bag was 50% off so i basically made money. girl math."

> "concert tickets bought in advance are free by the time the concert happens. i don't make the rules"
