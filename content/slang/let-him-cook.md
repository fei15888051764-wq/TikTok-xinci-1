Let him cook means get out of the way and let someone do what they're doing. No interruptions, no backseat driving, no "are you sure about that." The person is mid-process, the outcome isn't clear yet, and the phrase demands you respect the process.

Here's the twist that makes it interesting: it's used with total sincerity and total irony, sometimes in the same comment section. Your friend starts freestyle rapping and it's actually good? Let him cook. Your friend starts freestyle rapping and it's a trainwreck you can't look away from? Also let him cook. One is support. The other is popcorn.

## Where it actually started

Credit goes to Lil B, the Berkeley rapper who built an entire philosophy around cooking. In a 2010 YouTube video he explained it himself: to cook means taking your time and putting care into something, the way a master chef approaches a dish, and "let that boy cook" means let him do his thing. He even had a cooking dance, which looked less like cooking and more like stirring an imaginary pot, and he was very proud of it.

The phrase got its first mainstream sports boost through James Harden, who did a cooking celebration after big plays and reportedly got Lil B's blessing to use it, narrowly avoiding the infamous Based God's Curse that Lil B placed on players who stole the move without permission. Kevin Durant got cursed. Harden asked first. Legends understand licensing.

Then came the "Let Russ Cook" era. When Russell Wilson joined the Denver Broncos, fans and even the team's own social accounts begged the coaches to let Russ cook, meaning give him the offense and get out of his way. It was sincere. Then the season went badly, and "let Russ cook" became the sarcastic joke of the year, which taught everyone that the phrase's sincerity lives or dies with the results.

## How the meme form took over

The version that rules TikTok was born in December 2022: a Woody from Toy Story image, hand raised, captioned "Hold up, let him cook." It became a Twitch emote, a reply-guy staple, and the default response to any video where someone is about to attempt something ambitious. Dictionary.com put "let them cook" on its 2024 slang trends list, which is the linguistic equivalent of a retirement watch, except the phrase is still very much employed.

## Using it without sounding like a cop

The pronoun flexes freely. Let her cook, let them cook, let bro cook, all correct. The one rule: it only works when the outcome is still unknown. You say it during the attempt, not after. Once the result is in, you've missed the window and you look like you just discovered the phrase yesterday.

The spin-off to know: "never let bro cook again," deployed when the cooking produced something inedible. My group chat used it last month on a friend who DJ'd a house party with nothing but 2012 dubstep. Fair verdict, honestly.

And a boundary worth stating: don't let him cook when the person actually needs help. There's a difference between giving someone room and watching them set the kitchen on fire because you wanted content. If your friend is about to text his ex at 2 AM, that's not cooking. Intervene.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does let him cook mean on TikTok?

It means give someone space to do their thing without interrupting. On TikTok it's used both as genuine encouragement and as an ironic way to watch someone's idea play out, for better or worse.

### Is let him cook a compliment or an insult?

It depends entirely on the outcome you're expecting. Said with confidence, it's support. Said while grinning at an obvious disaster in progress, it's irony. The phrase itself is neutral; your face does the meaning.

### Who started let him cook?

Rapper Lil B, who explained the concept in a 2010 YouTube video as part of his cooking philosophy: taking time and care with your craft. The meme image of Woody from Toy Story turned it into a mass-market reply in late 2022.

### Can I say let her cook or let them cook?

Absolutely. The pronoun is fully flexible and always has been. Let her cook, let them cook, let us cook, all carry the same energy.
