## What Does 'Bebot' Mean? The TikTok Slang Explained (2026)

By **GEBILAOWANG** | Published: July 2, 2026

"Bebot" is a 2025-2026 TikTok slang term derived from Filipino culture meaning a beautiful, confident woman — essentially a "Filipina baddie." Popularized by the Black Eyed Peas' 2005 song of the same name, it went viral on TikTok in late 2025 through makeup transformation videos celebrating Y2K-era Filipina beauty and cultural pride.

**How a 20-Year-Old Filipino Slang Word Became TikTok's Hottest Beauty Trend**

The term "bebot" has deep roots in Filipino slang, dating back to at least the 1980s as a casual term of endearment for an attractive woman — equivalent to "babe," "boo," or "hot chick" in English. It gained international recognition in 2005 when Black Eyed Peas member apl.de.ap (a Filipino-American rapper) released the song "Bebot" as a tribute to his heritage, performing entirely in Tagalog. The word stayed largely within Filipino communities for two decades until December 27, 2025, when Filipina creator Belle Pauleen (@bellepauleen) posted a TikTok video captioned "bebot is just Filipina baddie but in Tagalog," dancing to the Black Eyed Peas track while showcasing full glam makeup. The video struck a chord, and by early February 2026, the trend had exploded globally — creators from the U.S., South Korea, and beyond were posting "bebot" transformation videos, evolving the trend to emphasize "historically accurate bebots" with heavy bronzer, thin eyebrows, metallic eyeshadows, and glossy lips reminiscent of early-2000s Filipina beauty icons. The Black Eyed Peas themselves acknowledged the trend's resurgence, with will.i.am posting on TikTok: "Filipinos should have their anthem as well."

**Why "Bebot" Represents a Deeper Cultural Shift on TikTok**

The term captures a fascinating cultural reclamation: a word that was originally used casually within Filipino communities is now being celebrated and aestheticized by a global Gen Z audience. Unlike earlier instances of cultural appropriation where elements were taken without context, the "bebot" trend is largely driven by Filipina creators themselves — Belle Pauleen, Glorianna Velez (@gloriannavelez), and Elliz (@xelliz) have been central to shaping the trend's aesthetics and meaning. GEBILAOWANG's take: what makes "bebot" significant is that it represents a shift in how cultural slang travels online. Rather than being diluted or appropriated, the term is being celebrated with its cultural context intact. The makeup tutorials don't just use the word — they explicitly connect it to Filipino beauty standards, Y2K nostalgia, and cultural pride. In an era where TikTok trends often flatten cultural specificity into generic aesthetics, "bebot" stands out as an example of cultural expression that retains its roots while going global.

**FAQ**

- **Q:** What older expression is this most similar to? How is it different?
  **A:** "Bebot" is closest to "baddie" or "bombshell" — terms that describe an attractive, confident woman. However, "bebot" specifically carries Filipino cultural connotations and celebrates a particular aesthetic rooted in Filipina beauty standards. Unlike "baddie," which has become somewhat generic, "bebot" retains cultural specificity.

- **Q:** Can this word accidentally offend someone?
  **A:** When used by non-Filipinos, "bebot" can be seen as cultural appropriation if used without understanding its context. The safest approach is to appreciate the trend as a celebration of Filipina culture rather than adopting the term casually. Filipina creators have generally welcomed genuine appreciation and participation.

- **Q:** Is this a passing trend or here to stay?
