**"Fanum Tax"** is the unspoken law of friend groups: whoever ordered the food has to surrender the first bite — no exceptions, no appeals.

It started as a TikTok streamer's personal bit and became so universal that Merriam-Webster officially added it to the dictionary. The rule is simple, unfair, and absolutely non-negotiable. If you didn't buy it, you tax it.

## Usage & Context

**"Fanum Tax" is a playful rule where the person who buys the food takes a mandatory bite of everyone else's meal before handing it over, popularized by TikTok streamer Fanum.**

The phrase works in two contexts. In its original sense, it describes the specific bit where Fanum (and now millions of people copying him) grabs a bite of a friend's burger, fry, or pizza slice and declares it "Fanum Tax" — as if collecting a government-mandated tariff on all food transactions. In its broader sense, it's become shorthand for any situation where someone takes a small share of what belongs to someone else, framed as a joke rather than theft.

On TikTok, you'll see it in POV videos where creators act out the "tax collector" role, snatching food from friends with dramatic flair. In group chats, someone will drop "Fanum Tax" as a caption when they steal a fry off your plate. The phrase has even spread to non-food contexts — borrowing a pen, using someone's charger, or taking the aux cord all get labeled as "Fanum Tax" if done with the right comedic timing.

> **Group chat:** "just took a bite of your sandwich. Fanum Tax applies"

> **TikTok POV:** *grabs fry from friend's McDonald's bag* "The government requires its share"

> **Instagram caption:** "Birthday cake? Fanum Tax. First slice goes to me, I bought it"

> **Reply:** "my little brother applies Fanum Tax to everything in the fridge and I can't do anything about it"

## Origin Story

The term originated in 2022 from TikTok streamer Fanum, whose real name is Roberto Gonzalez. During his livestreams, Fanum developed a running gag where he would grab food from his friends' plates and justify it as collecting a "tax." The bit was so consistent and naturally funny that his audience started using "Fanum Tax" to describe any food-related petty theft.

The phrase exploded into mainstream culture in 2023 when it was referenced in the viral TikTok song "Sticking Out Your Gyatt for the Rizzler," which name-dropped both "Fanum Tax" and "skibidi toilet" in the same breath. The song became an inescapable earworm across the platform, embedding "Fanum Tax" into Gen Z vocabulary whether people knew who Fanum was or not.

Merriam-Webster officially added "Fanum Tax" to the dictionary on June 30th, 2026 — a remarkable achievement for a term born on TikTok just four years earlier. The dictionary defined it as "a playful rule where the person who buys the food takes a mandatory bite of everyone else's meal before handing it over."

## Cultural Significance

"Fanum Tax" represents a broader trend of streamer culture reshaping everyday language. Before streaming, inside jokes stayed inside friend groups. Now, a streamer's personal bit can become a dictionary entry within four years — because millions of people watched it happen live, adopted the phrase, and used it enough that lexicographers had to take notice.

The phrase also captures a specific Gen Z and Gen Alpha humor pattern: reframing petty behavior as institutional policy. Calling food theft a "tax" transforms a minor annoyance into a bureaucratic inevitability, which makes it funnier. It's the same comedic logic behind "Admin Tax" (charging your friends for emotional labor) and "Sibling Tax" (the universal right of older siblings to claim a portion of younger siblings' snacks).
