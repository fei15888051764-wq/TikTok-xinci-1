Low taper fade is a real haircut: short on the sides, gradually longer on top, fade starting just above the ears. Barbers love it. But when someone on TikTok says "low taper fade" out of nowhere, they're not asking for grooming advice. They're quoting one of the internet's most stubborn memes, a joke about a haircut that has somehow stayed alive for years through sheer repetition.

The meme works as a non-sequitur: a random "imagine if Ninja got a low taper fade" dropped into an unrelated comment section, a serious video, a wedding toast. There's no setup and no punchline beyond the phrase itself. The haircut is the joke. That's the whole technology.

## The freestyle that started it

On January 5, 2024, the musician and streamer ericdoa went live on Twitch and freestyled for about an hour, testing hooks for a song. Somewhere around the 29-minute mark he sang the immortal line: "but imagine if Ninja got a low taper fade." Ninja, for anyone blessed enough not to know, is Tyler Blevins, possibly the most famous gamer alive, and at the time he had never publicly worn that haircut.

The clip went up on TikTok days later and pulled over a million views in a day. Then the internet did the one thing guaranteed to supercharge a joke: Ninja himself went out on January 13 and actually got a low taper fade, posting the reveal with ericdoa's original sound. That video cleared 5 million views in three days. Life imitated the freestyle, and the meme graduated from "funny clip" to "cultural reference point."

## Ninja is dragging it, and that's the sequel

Most memes die quietly. This one got a second life from its own death. As the joke aged, a new genre emerged: Ninja "dragging" the low taper fade meme, meaning insisting it's still popular long after everyone else moved on. Clips and edits of him keeping the faith became their own meme format, with variations spreading through 2024 and 2025, and the joke's refusal to die became funnier than the joke.

That's the layer people are on now. Saying "low taper fade" in 2026 is partly quoting ericdoa, partly mocking Ninja's loyalty to the bit, and partly just enjoying that a haircut has outlasted most careers. The meme is dragging itself.

There's a whole economy of this stuff now. Meme pages run "day 800 of the low taper fade" counters. Barbers report real customers walking in and asking for the Ninja special, which means the joke has completed the rare full circle from haircut to meme and back to haircut. Most meme formats never achieve this. They get one week of glory and a quiet burial. This one got a second career, and nobody can fully explain why this specific haircut was chosen for immortality.

## How to use it

Two registers. First, the non-sequitur: drop "low taper fade" anywhere it doesn't belong, no explanation. Comments under a baking video, a funeral scene edit, a stock market analysis. The confusion is the product.

Second, the loyalty bit: when someone declares a meme dead, respond that it's still massive, still dragging, fresh cut and all. Bonus points for defending it with the confidence of a man who just left the barbershop.

Third, the escalation: photoshop the fade onto increasingly unqualified candidates. Historical figures. House pets. The moon. If the moon had hair, it would have a low taper fade by now.

One warning from experience: the joke needs an audience that already knows it. I watched someone try it at an actual barbershop and the barber just nodded and asked for a reference photo. Wrong crowd. Devastating.
