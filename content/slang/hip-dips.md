## Pronunciation
/hɪp dɪps/

## Multi-angle Interpretation

"Hip dips" refers to the natural inward curves that occur just below the hip bones, where the skin follows the shape of the pelvis. Medically known as "trochanteric depressions," these indentations are a completely normal anatomical feature determined by bone structure, not weight or fitness level.

The TikTok trend around hip dips emerged as a reaction against the unrealistic body standards perpetuated by social media. For years, fitness influencers and wellness brands had marketed exercises and products claiming to "eliminate hip dips," framing them as a flaw. The body-positive counter-movement on TikTok sought to educate people that hip dips are simply how human bodies are built.

GEBILAOWANG's take: the "hip dips" trend is part of a broader cultural shift toward anatomical literacy and body acceptance. It's not just a trend—it's a corrective to years of misinformation that made people feel insecure about a feature they literally cannot change (since it's determined by bone structure). The linguistic aspect is interesting too: giving something a cute, accessible name makes it easier to discuss and destigmatize.

## Origin & Usage

The term "hip dips" has been used in fashion and fitness circles for years, but its TikTok virality began around 2024 when body-positive creators started posting educational content about the feature. The trend gained significant momentum when medical professionals and physical therapists began corroborating the message that hip dips are anatomically normal.

The term's success lies in its accessibility. "Trochanteric depressions" is clinical and intimidating; "hip dips" is friendly and memorable. This naming strategy has helped the body-positive message reach audiences who might otherwise dismiss it as medical jargon.

**Applicable crowds:** Body-positive advocates, fitness professionals, anyone with a body (so, everyone)

**Usage taboos:** Don't use "hip dips" as an insult or suggest that having them (or not having them) makes someone more or less attractive. The whole point of the trend is that this feature is neutral—neither good nor bad, just normal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can exercise get rid of hip dips?**
A: No. Hip dips are caused by the shape of your pelvis and femur—the distance between your hip bone and thigh bone creates the indentation. Since you can't change your bone structure through exercise, you can't eliminate hip dips. You can build muscle in the area, which might slightly change the appearance, but the dip will remain.

**Q: Are hip dips attractive or unattractive?**
A: That's entirely subjective, and the question itself misses the point. Hip dips are a neutral anatomical feature, like having a collarbone or kneecaps. Whether someone finds them attractive is personal preference, not an objective standard.

**Q: Why did people ever think hip dips were bad?**
A: The wellness industry historically framed any body feature that didn't match an idealized hourglass shape as a "problem" to be solved—often through products or services they were selling. Hip dips became a target simply because they don't fit the artificial standard, not because there's anything actually wrong with them.
